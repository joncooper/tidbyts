## four-screens

[conversational]
I have four little pixel displays around my office. They used to show weather
and scores. I gave them a second life. Now they tell me what Codex is doing,
what just finished, and when something actually needs me. They ask almost
nothing of me: no phone, no tab, and no notification buzzing in my pocket.

## the-idea

I call it Tidbyts: a quiet status wall for Codex, built from the original
Tidbyt displays. Each screen does one job. Instead of cramming in a dashboard,
it shows one useful fact I can read from across the room. At this size, you
cannot hide behind charts. The screen has to know what matters.

## the-local-agent

Codex has two jobs here. First, it helped me build Tidbyts. Then it became the
recurring local automation behind the pixels. Every fifteen minutes, it wakes
up on my Mac, checks my coding tasks and the computer, turns the results into
fresh screens, and sends them to the displays. Most of the time it finds
nothing urgent, which is exactly what I want.

## visible-work

Control Tower tells me what Codex is working on, what happened recently, which
background jobs are still running, and what needs a reply. Glint turns that same
information into a tiny character—working, ready, or celebrating a finish—so I
can see progress without being pulled back into another screen.

## evolving-monitor

[with quiet emphasis]
The display I care about most is Exception Screen. When everything is healthy,
it stays quiet. But if a software build fails, free disk space gets low, a
watched service disappears, cloud data goes stale, or spending approaches a
limit, that problem takes over the screen. Because the Codex automation runs
locally, I can teach it a new check whenever my setup changes. The pixels stay
simple even as the monitor behind them grows. This week that might be a
software build. Next month it could be a new service or a budget.

## more-signals

Other displays track finished work, AI usage, billable hours, and even a
household clean-out. The private part stays private: prompts, code, files, and
transcripts never leave my Mac. The optional shared service gets only small,
content-free usage records, merged-PR totals, and a household score.

## build-week

I built Tidbyts for OpenAI Build Week with Codex running GPT-5.6. Codex helped
connect the checks on my Mac to the software that draws the screens, then helped
test the awkward edges of a display just sixty-four pixels wide. That was its
first job. Its second is the recurring automation that runs the wall.

## close

[warmly]
Four old Tidbyts, one local Codex automation, and far fewer trips through
dashboards. When something needs me, I just look up.
